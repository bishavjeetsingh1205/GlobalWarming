Causes for Global Warming

"See yourself first, then blame others"

Global warming, the long-term increase in Earth's average surface temperature, is primarily driven by a complex interplay of human activities and natural processes. Though this warming trend has been going on for a long time, its pace has significantly increased in the last hundred years due to the burning of fossil fuels. As the human population has increased, so has the volume of fossil fuels burned. Fossil fuels include coal, oil, and natural gas, and burning them causes what is known as the “greenhouse effect” in Earth atmosphere. The greenhouse effect is when the sun rays penetrate the atmosphere, but when that heat is reflected off the surface cannot escape back into space. Gases produced by the burning of fossil fuels prevent the heat from leaving the atmosphere. These greenhouse gasses are carbon dioxide, chlorofluorocarbons(CFCs), water vapor, methane, and nitrous oxide. The excess heat in the atmosphere has caused the average global temperature to rise overtime, otherwise known as global warming.


The most significant human-induced cause is the release of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere, with carbon dioxide (CO2) being the primary culprit. This stems from the burning of fossil fuels for energy and transportation, as well as industrial processes, which collectively contribute to a substantial CO2 buildup. Methane emissions from sources like agriculture, livestock, and natural gas production are another critical factor, as methane has a much higher heat-trapping potential than CO2 in the short term. Deforestation and land-use changes lead to the release of stored carbon in trees and soil. Industrial activities, such as cement production, also emit CO2. Moreover, agricultural practices involving synthetic fertilizers and rice cultivation release methane into the atmosphere. Landfills contribute methane emissions when organic waste decomposes without oxygen. The removal of forests and natural vegetation reduces the Earth's capacity to absorb carbon dioxide through the process of photosynthesis. This results in the release of stored carbon, further increasing atmospheric CO2 levels. Additionally, land-use changes such as urbanization and agriculture not only reduce the planet's ability to absorb carbon but also increase the albedo, or reflectivity, of the Earth's surface. Albedo changes contribute to local and regional warming by affecting the way solar radiation is absorbed and reflected, certain synthetic fluorinated gases used in refrigeration and manufacturing, known as "F-gases", have a high global warming potential. The transportation sector relies on gasoline and diesel fuels, further adding to CO2 emissions. Meanwhile, the energy sector's dependence on fossil fuels exacerbates global warming, as does energy consumption in industries and residential settings. Pollution, such as black carbon settling on ice and snow, reduces their reflectivity and accelerating melting.

Sometimes these phrases are used interchangeably, however, they are different. Climate change refers to changes in weather patterns and growing seasons around the world. It also refers to sea level rise caused by the expansion of warmer seas and melting ice sheets and glaciers. Global warming causes climate change, which poses a serious threat to life on Earth in the forms of widespread flooding and extreme weather.
